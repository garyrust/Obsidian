Policy

ARD Policy on Repeater Information and Factual Data

Purpose

This document describes how the Amateur Repeater Directory (ARD) treats repeater information and outlines the generally accepted distinction between factual operating data and copyrightable content as applied in amateur radio practice.

How ARD Represents Repeater Information

ARD provides a factual, informational view of amateur radio repeater systems and related operating data. ARD does not assign authority, grant permission, or supersede existing amateur radio organizations. It represents observable repeater characteristics and operating context to improve visibility, understanding, and practical usability for operators and communities.

Repeater Operating Information

A repeater is configured and operated by an individual or organization (the repeater owner). The repeater owner determines the operational parameters required to access the system, including:

  • Output frequency
  • Input frequency or offset
  • Access method (e.g., CTCSS/DCS tone or digital equivalent)

These parameters are functional operating characteristics. They describe how a repeater operates and how it may be accessed on the air. They are not creative works or editorial content. ARD treats repeater operating parameters as factual descriptions of real-world radio systems.

Frequency Allocation and Use

Amateur radio spectrum is allocated by the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) and is made available for use within the amateur service.

  • Amateur frequencies are not privately owned
  • Use of spectrum is governed by regulation and operating practice
  • Operating parameters describe usage, not ownership

ARD includes frequency and access information solely as factual descriptions of repeater operation.

Repeater Coordination

Frequency coordination within amateur radio is a voluntary and advisory practice carried out by independent, non-regulatory organizations.

There is no requirement that a repeater be coordinated in order to operate in compliance with FCC rules. Repeaters may operate as:

  • Coordinated
  • Uncoordinated
  • Temporarily coordinated
  • De-coordinated

Coordination status is treated by ARD as one attribute of a repeater and does not determine legitimacy or legality.

Coordinated and Uncoordinated Repeaters

Uncoordinated repeaters are a normal and longstanding part of amateur radio practice.

An uncoordinated repeater is not inherently illegal, unauthorized, or invalid. Any informational system that represents amateur radio infrastructure must account for both coordinated and uncoordinated systems in order to reflect real-world operating conditions accurately.

ARD includes both coordinated and uncoordinated repeaters where information is available.

Factual Data and Copyright

In general, U.S. copyright principles distinguish between facts and creative expression.

Repeater operating parameters such as frequencies, offsets, access tones, callsigns, and geographic locations represent factual, functional information. They describe observable characteristics of radio systems rather than original creative works.

Directories and organizations may hold rights to their presentation, editorial commentary, or proprietary annotations. These rights do not extend to ownership of the underlying operational facts of a repeater system. ARD respects intellectual property while representing factual operating data.

Data Stewardship

Repeater information included in ARD may be derived from:

  • Repeater owner submissions
  • Community contributions
  • Independent verification
  • Publicly observable operating facts

ARD’s role is to represent information accurately and transparently, not to assert exclusivity or control.

Minimum Information for Repeater Access

From an operating standpoint, the minimum information required to access a repeater is:

  1. Output frequency
  2. Input frequency or offset
  3. Access method (if required)

Additional information—such as coverage estimates, coordination status, or network affiliation—provides context and usability but does not establish ownership or exclusivity.

Summary

  • ARD provides a factual, informational view of repeater systems and operating data
  • Repeater operating parameters are functional descriptions of system behavior
  • Amateur frequencies are publicly allocated and not privately owned
  • Repeater coordination is voluntary and advisory
  • Coordinated and uncoordinated repeaters coexist as standard practice
  • Repeater owners remain the authoritative source of their system configuration

ARD’s goal is accurate representation and practical understanding of amateur radio infrastructure as it exists in real-world operation.

Footnote: This policy describes ARD’s data stewardship approach and reflects established amateur radio practice. It is informational in nature and does not provide legal advice.

Seeding

ARD Data Seeding and Transition Policy

Purpose

This document describes how the Amateur Repeater Directory (ARD) was initially populated and how its dataset is intentionally evolving over time. It exists to set clear expectations around data provenance, transition, and long-term stewardship, and to explain how ARD prioritizes accuracy, relevance, and real-world operating value.

Initial Data Seeding

During its early development, ARD was initially seeded using publicly available sources to establish baseline geographic coverage and to demonstrate the technical practicality of the platform.

This initial seeding was a transitional measure. Its purpose was to validate mapping, terrain analysis, range modeling, and operating workflows while the systems required for owner participation, verification, and ongoing maintenance were being built.

Seeded records were not intended to represent a final, authoritative, or permanent dataset.

Transition to Verified and Active Records

ARD is transitioning from seeded records toward a curated dataset based on:

  • Repeater owner participation
  • Community review and validation
  • Observable on-air activity
  • Association with active nets, clubs, and real operating use

As this transition progresses, seeded records are phased out and replaced by repeaters that are actively maintained and meaningfully used.

How Updates Work

ARD is designed to improve over time through clear, accountable update paths. Repeater information may be added or updated in three ways:

  • Registered Users (reviewed): Changes submitted by registered users enter a review process before being published.
  • Admins (no review required): Users granted Admin status can publish updates directly.
  • Repeater Owners (no approval after claim): Once a repeater is claimed and verified, the repeater owner can update that repeater without additional approval.

This model balances openness with accountability by enabling community participation while ensuring that verified owners and trusted admins can keep active systems current in real time.

Emphasis on Practical Value

ARD does not aim to maximize record count. It prioritizes practical operating value. A directory containing a smaller number of repeaters with confirmed ownership, active nets, and ongoing community participation provides greater usefulness than a much larger list that might contain unverified or inactive systems. Coverage alone is not the goal. Context, activity, and reliability matter more.

Data Stewardship Approach

ARD’s long-term approach emphasizes:

  • Repeater owners as the primary source of system information
  • Transparent change history and auditability
  • Community participation and accountability
  • Ongoing refinement rather than static listings

This approach allows the dataset to improve over time as participation increases and stale information is removed.

Ongoing Transition

The transition from seeded records to verified, actively maintained repeaters is an ongoing process. During this period, some records may exist in different stages of verification. ARD is designed to support this evolution openly and deliberately, with the expectation that quality and usefulness increase over time.

Summary

  • Initial data seeding established baseline coverage and demonstrated platform capability
  • Seeded records are transitional and are being phased out
  • Verified, active repeaters define long-term value
  • Practical operating relevance is prioritized over raw quantity
  • ARD evolves through participation, verification, and stewardship

ARD reflects amateur radio infrastructure as it exists in practice, with a focus on usefulness, transparency, and long-term reliability.

Keeping a Statewide Network Visible and Current

This page highlights a real, fixable data challenge in amateur radio emergency communications. The goal is not to criticize, but to show how easily a more accurate, up-to-date public view of statewide networks can be created using tools that already exist today.

In Florida, the Statewide Amateur Radio Network (SARnet) represents a serious, real-world emergency communications system. It is on the air, used by operators, and relied on during emergencies. The challenge is not that SARnet does not exist — it clearly does — but that its public-facing data is difficult to keep current and easy for other systems to misinterpret.

SARnet’s publicly available repeater listing, last published in 2023, contains only minimal technical detail. It provides enough information to suggest where the network operates, but not enough to stay synchronized with coordination databases or modern repeater directories. The original document can be viewed here.

SARnet also maintains a system status page, updated as recently as June 1, 2025, which confirms overall network health. That page is available here. While helpful, it does not expose site-level technical data that could be shared or embedded elsewhere, which makes it difficult for outside systems to reflect SARnet accurately.

To better understand how this affects visibility, the SARnet frequency and tone list was compared against the latest FASMA coordinated and uncoordinated repeater data. The comparison was limited to Florida, UHF repeaters, and exact frequency and PL tone matches.

Only two SARnet sites currently appear in the FASMA data with matching frequency, matching PL tone, and a linked network flag.

This does not mean the rest of SARnet is “missing.” It means the network cannot be reliably identified as a network in the public data most people rely on. That creates confusion for operators, clubs, and agencies who are simply trying to understand what systems are available.

Because there is no authoritative, machine-readable public definition of the SARnet network, the embedded list below does not claim to be “the official SARnet.” Instead, it shows what can be verified today using observable data and public records. The purpose is to demonstrate the gap — and how easily it can be closed.

This list is live. During testing, marking a repeater offline in ARD and refreshing a club website immediately reflected the change. This same mechanism could be used to power a live SARnet status or repeater page without manual updates.

How This Helps Everyone

Coordination bodies, emergency networks, clubs, and operators all want the same thing: accurate, current information that reflects what is actually on the air. Keeping that data synchronized across multiple platforms is hard, especially when updates are manual.

ARD is designed to make that easier. Networks can be defined once, embedded anywhere, and kept current automatically. When something changes, the update is visible immediately and subscribers can be notified in real time.

The opportunity here is simple. With minimal effort, SARnet could have a live, self-updating public presence that reflects its real-world operations and reduces confusion for everyone who depends on it.

Local Net Admins - We're Recruiting

Help keep local nets easy to find — and keep the details accurate for operators who rely on them.

What Is a Local Net Admin?

A Local Net Admin is a trusted volunteer who is given Net Admin access on the Amateur Repeater Directory website. You’ll help maintain net listings for a defined club, region, or coverage area — so operators can quickly find real, active nets.

Admin access is enabled after a quick review to help keep the directory accurate and trusted.

What You’ll Do

  • Add and maintain net listings for your area
  • Confirm schedules and details (time, frequency, tone, notes)
  • Update listings when nets move, change, or go silent
  • Mark special-event or seasonal nets appropriately
  • Help clubs keep their nets visible by keeping listings current

Who Should Apply

Best fit for people already active locally, such as:

  • Club officers or leadership members
  • Regular net control operators
  • Repeater trustees or coordinators
  • Licensed Operators and Owners
