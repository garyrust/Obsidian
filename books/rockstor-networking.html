You can technically aggregate the two 2.5 Gb Ethernet ports on your Asustor AS5202T
NAS units, but given that they have mechanical hard drives and you want to move large

files, you should not use traditional link aggregation (LACP) if you primarily access the

NAS from a single computer. Instead, you should use SMB Multichannel. ¢

Why Link Aggregation (LACP) is not ideal for your use case

Traditional link aggregation (using a managed switch that supports LACP) is designed for
multi-user environments, not single clients.

  • Single Client Bottleneck: With LACP, a single file transfer (session) is still limited to
    the speed of a single link, which is 2.5 Gbps (~280 MB/s). You won't get the combined
    5 Gbps speed (~560 MB/s) for one large file transfer.

  • Mechanical HDD Limit: The main issue is that a single mechanical hard disk drive
    often has a real-world maximum transfer rate of around 150-200 MB/s anyway, which
    means even a single 2.5 Gbps link is likely sufficient and may not even be fully utilized.
    The drives will be your primary bottleneck before the networkis.

The Recommended Solution: SMB Multichannel 2

Asustor NAS devices support SMB Multichannel, which allows a single client to use both
Ethernet ports simultaneously over multiple TCP connections, effectively achieving higher
speeds for a single file transfer.

  • How it Works: SMB Multichannel is a feature of the SMB protocol (used by Windows
    and modern macOS) that allows a client to use multiple network connections to a
    single server (your NAS).

  • Setup: The best part is that it often doesn't require a special managed switch. You just
    need to connect both 2.5 Gb ports on the NAS to your (currently 1 Gbps) switch, or
    even better, a new 2.5 GbE or multi-gig switch, and ensure SMB Multichannel is
    enabled in the NAS settings.

  • Benefit: This allows a single client computer (once upgraded with a 2.5 GbE NIC, as
    discussed previously) to potentially utilize the full combined bandwidth of both ports,
    although you'll still be limited by the mechanical drive speeds. 2

Summary

  1. Do not use LACP Link Aggregation for single-client speed improvements.

2, Enable SMB Multichannel in your Asustor ADM settings for the best potential single-
client performance with large files.

  1. Upgrade your PC's Ethernet to 2.5 GbE (or higher) to take advantage of the NAS's
    ports.

4, Consider a multi-gig switch to connect everything at 2.5 Gb speeds or higher. ¢

If you are running Rockstor (Linux-based) and using NFS for file sharing, the rules
change significantly compared to standard Asustor firmware.

The Short Answer

No, do not aggregate (LACP/Bonding) if your goal is to speed up a single file transfer to

  1. The NFS Limitation

Unlike SMB, NFS (v3 and v4.1) does not support "Multichannel" easily for a single
client. ¢

Bonding/LACP: Even if you bond the two 2.5G ports on your NAS, a single NFS
transfer between your PC and the NAS will still stay on a single 2.5G path. It will not
combine to 5G for one file Red Hat Customer Portal - Bonding.

  • The Drive Bottleneck: Since you have mechanical HDDs, a single 2.5G link (280
    MB/s) is already faster than what a single HDD can usually provide (150-200 MBI).
    Even in a RAID mirror, your drives will likely "max out" before the 2.5G network
    does. ¢
  1. How to use both ports with Rockstor

Since you are using Rockstor (based on openSUSE), you have two main options for those
dual ports:

  • Option A: Active-Backup (Redundancy)
    If one cable fails or one port on your future switch dies, the NAS stays online. This
    doesn't increase speed, but it increases reliability. You can set this up in the Rockstor
    WebUI under the Network tab.
  • Option B: Separate Networks (Isolation)
    You could put one 2.5G port on a dedicated "Storage Network" (just for backups
    between the two NASes) and the other on your "Home Network" (for your PC). This
    ensures that if you are syncing files between the two AS5202T units, it doesn't slow
    down your PC's access.

  1. The Better Way to Speed Up NFS
    If you want the fastest performance on Rockstor with NFS:

  2. Skip Bonding: It adds complexity (and requires a managed switch) for almost no gain
    in your specific setup.

  3. Enable Jumbo Frames: If your future 2.5G switch and your PC support it, set MTU to
    9000 on both the NAS and PC. This reduces CPU overhead and can squeeze more
    performance out of mechanical drives over NFS.

  4. Optimize Export Options: In Rockstor, ensure your NFS exports use async if you
    value speed over absolute data safety during a power outage, or use sync with an
    SSD cache (Log device). ¢

Summary for your AS5202T: Since your drives are the bottleneck and NFS doesn't
"stripe" across links like SMB Multichannel does, keep it simple. Use one 2.5G port for
your main traffic and use the second port only if you want a dedicated “sync link" directly
between the two NASes.

sync data between the two NAS units

Since you are syncing between two AS5202T units running Rockstor, you have a unique
opportunity to create a "High-Speed Syne Tunnel” that bypasses your main network
traffic entirely.

  1. The "Direct-Connect" Strategy (No Switch Needed)

Because you have two ports on each NAS, you can use one port for your regular network
and the second port for a direct 2.5 Gb connection between the two units.

  • Physical Connection: Plug a Caté cable directly from Port 2 of NAS-A into Port 2 of
    NAS-B.

  • IP Configuration: Assign these ports a different static IP subnet (e.g., 10.0.0.1 and
    10.0.0.2).

  • The Benefit: When you run your sync (via rsync, btrfs send/receive , or
    Rockstor’s replication), point the destination to the 10.0.0.x address. The data will fly
    across that dedicated 2.5G cable without eating up bandwidth on your main
    router/switch or affecting your PC’s internet speed.
  1. Why not "Aggregate" here?

Aggregation (Bonding) is overkill for this for two reasons:

  1. Complexity: LACP bonding usually requires a managed switch. A direct cable is free
    and faster to set up.

  2. The HDD Bottleneck: Your mechanical drives are the speed limit. A single 2.5G port
    handles ~280 MB/s. Most mechanical drives in a NAS top out at ~180-220 MB/s. Adding
    a second 2.56 link (to make 5G) won't make the physical spinning platters move data
    any faster.

  3. Making the Sync Faster on Rockstor

Since Rockstor uses BTRFS, don't just use standard tools. Use the power of the
filesystem:

  • BTRFS Send/Receive: If you are syncing entire shares, this is significantly faster than
    rsync because it only sends the changed blocks at the filesystem level. You can set
    this up through the Rockstor Replication tool.

  • Compression: Ensure Zstd compression is enabled on your shares. It reduces the
    amount of data that actually needs to be written to the mechanical disks, often making
    the sync feel faster than the network limit.

  1. Summary Recommendation
  • Don't Aggregate. It won't help your mechanical drive speeds.

irect Connect Port 2 to Port 2. Use a dedicated cable between the NASes for a
"clean" 2.5G sync path.

  • Use BTRFS Replication. Use the native Rockstor tools to move data over that
    dedicated link.
